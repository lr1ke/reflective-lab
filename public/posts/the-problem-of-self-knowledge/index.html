The Problem of Self-Knowledge

Sense, Reference, and the Transforming Self.

This paper establishes four results. (1) The popular thesis that the self is transformed by the acquisition of self-knowledge, when combined with the requirement that knowledge accumulate about a single object, is inconsistent — and inconsistent under every assignment of the transformation: to the subject of knowledge, to the object, or to a knower and known taken as identical. (2) Frege’s distinction between sense and reference, and in particular his account of informative identity, resolves the inconsistency: what multiplies in self-knowledge is not the object but its senses; the reference — the self — must be held fixed. (3) From the Fregean account two adequacy conditions on self-knowledge follow: there must be a single reference object, and it must admit multiple senses. These conditions yield truth conditions for self-knowledge claims and, with them, a determinate way in which the attempt at self-knowledge can fail: a derived representation about the self taken for a primitive mode of its givenness. (4) It follows that narrative accounts of personal identity which dispense with a stable referent — Ricœur’s ipse without idem, and its relatives — forfeit the claim that self-narration constitutes knowledge: without a reference, the narrative identity statement has no truth conditions, and its composition is arbitrary.

1. Introduction#

“Know thyself.”

For more than two thousand years this injunction has been regarded as one of philosophy’s deepest demands.

Yet it conceals a remarkable difficulty. One way into this difficulty is to begin with a popular thesis in contemporary philosophy: that the self is transformed by the very act of coming to know itself.

In recent philosophy L. A. Paul has given it a precise form: a genuinely transformative experience changes the kind of person you take yourself to be, so that “there may be no first-personal view of the self that is invariant under these epistemic transformations.”1 The thesis is older. Comte raised it against the possibility of introspection: “the organ observed and the organ observing being, in this case, identical, how could observation take place?”2 Schwitzgebel states it as an observation about attention: as one turns attention onto an experience in order to know it, “it alters and grows, or it crumbles.”3 Each formulation makes the same claim: the act of knowing the self modifies the self.

If self-knowledge changes the very self it attempts to know, how can knowledge of oneself ever accumulate?

2. The Paradox of Transformation and Accumulation#

2.1. Three assumptions and two readings#

Fix the following terminology and assumptions.

(D1) Self-knowledge is knowledge in which the knower and the known are the same self.

(T) Transformation thesis: every act of acquiring self-knowledge changes the self.

(K) Accumulation condition: items of knowledge form a growing body of knowledge of one object only if they are about one and the same object.

(K) is not a special assumption about the self; it states what it is for knowledge to accumulate rather than merely to multiply. Two items of knowledge about two distinct objects do not constitute more knowledge of either.

One word in (K) requires a guard. “Object” there means the target of the items — what they are about. It is not the “object” of the subject/object schema, the known-pole of the knowing relation. For ordinary knowledge the two coincide harmlessly; in self-knowledge they can come apart, as §2.3.2 will show, and the ambiguity is resolved at full depth in §3.3.

The problem in its bare form: if the knower and the known are one and the same entity (D1), and if by every newly gained piece of knowledge about the self the self changes (T), then there is no expanding of knowledge of one and the same object, but only a growing manifold of concepts of self that are mutually unconnected — unconnected because the object of reference has changed between them. By (K), this manifold is not accumulated self-knowledge.

The thesis (T) admits two readings, and the argument must track both:

(T-r) Replacement reading: the self that results from the act is not identical with the self that entered it.

(T-a) Alteration reading: the same self persists through the act, but in an altered state.

Rather than settle the problem by rejecting (T) outright, I proceed by cases. The transformation can be assigned to the subject of knowledge (§2.3.1), to the object of knowledge (§2.3.2), or to a knower and known taken as strictly identical, with no subject/object structure at all (§2.3.3). Each case will be shown to fail, and to fail for the same reason (§2.4). The resolution is then constructed in §3, and its consequences drawn in §4.

2.2. Two candidates for the self#

The cases require a value for “the self.” Two candidates will serve.

(S1) The biological individual: the embodied person, a material object located in space and time.

(S2) The pure “I”: the transcendental I — the self in its purest form, prior to and independent of every mode in which it is given to itself. It is not a form of consciousness and not a bearer of content; it is the bare subject itself, an a priori, and not necessarily embodied. Its full characterization — as the fixed reference of self-knowledge, whose a priori status is earned structurally — is given in §3.3.

These are not the only conceivable candidates, and nothing in what follows requires that they be. What matters is this: the verdict of the case analysis of §2.3 is invariant under the choice between (S1) and (S2). The nature of the self enters the arguments at exactly one point: the replacement reading of (T) is a live option only for a candidate without material persistence conditions, and §2.3.1 records this — with the effect of closing one route for the embodied candidate the sooner, not of opening one. Beyond that point the arguments appeal only to the assumption that knowing the self changes it, and the paradox does not depend on any particular theory of what the self is. The choice between (S1) and (S2) becomes relevant again only in §3.3, where each candidate is installed, in turn, in the role the resolution requires.

2.3. The case analysis#

2.3.1. Case I — Transformation of the Subject#

Assume the transforming self is the subject of knowledge — the knower. The two readings of (T) yield two different arguments, and they divide the candidates of §2.2 between them.

Under (T-r). Fix first what the replacement reading does and does not assert. It asserts non-identity and nothing more: the self that completes the act is not the self that began it. It does not specify the respect in which the subject has changed, and it does not describe a mechanism of replacement. The successive selves are accordingly distinguished only as terms of a sequence — a first self, a second, a third, indexed by the acts of self-knowledge — not by any stated difference between them.

This abstractness has a consequence for the candidates, and it is a first result of the case analysis rather than a defect of the argument. For the pure “I” (S2), which has no material persistence conditions, the replacement reading is coherent: nothing in the concept of a pure subject excludes its being succeeded rather than altered. For the biological individual (S1) it is not: if the self is the organism, non-identity of the self is non-identity of the organism, and the reading asserts that every act of self-knowledge brings a new biological individual into existence — which is false. The replacement reading of (T) is therefore not available to a transformation theorist about the embodied self; such a theorist must intend the alteration reading, treated below. The following argument is addressed to candidates for which (T-r) is a live reading.

The argument. Suppose a knower N performs a successful act of self-knowledge at time t₁, and suppose, for contradiction, that the same knower N performs a second successful act at t₂ > t₁. For N to perform the second act, N must exist at t₂. But by (T-r), the self that exists after t₁ is not identical with N. So N performs no second act. Contradiction. Hence no knower performs more than one act of self-knowledge, and every gained item of self-knowledge must be ascribed to a distinct knower in an unboundedly growing sequence. Since every item of self-knowledge is about its own knower — this is (D1) — the items are about pairwise distinct objects, and by (K) they do not accumulate: each self in the sequence gains exactly one item, and no self inherits any.

One remark on the structure of this result. A plurality of momentary selves is a familiar position: Hume’s bundle, James’s succession of passing thoughts each appropriating its predecessors.4 But for Hume and James the plurality is a finding — the outcome of looking inward and failing to find an abiding thing. Here it is derived: it follows deductively from (T-r) and (D1). The difference matters, because a finding can be reinterpreted, whereas a derivation can only be blocked by rejecting a premise. Which premise must be rejected is the subject of §2.4.

Under (T-a). The subject persists through the act, in an altered state — and (T) does not specify the respect of the alteration. Suppose the knower gains an item of self-knowledge k₁ at t₁; k₁ describes the subject as it stood at t₁. By (T-a), the act alters the subject. Since the respect of the alteration is unspecified, it cannot be excluded that the alteration concerns exactly what k₁ asserts. Whether k₁ still holds of the altered subject is therefore an open question, and k₁ itself cannot answer it. Closing it requires a further act: an examination of whether k₁ still holds. But this examination is itself an act of self-knowledge — its object is the subject’s own present state — and by (T-a) it alters the subject again, thereby reopening, for k₁ and for its own result alike, exactly the question it was performed to close. Every act that would secure the standing of previously gained self-knowledge undermines that standing anew, and no finite sequence of acts terminates the process. The structure is a vicious infinite regress: each step reproduces the deficiency it was meant to remove. The conclusion: under (T-a), at no time does the subject possess an item of self-knowledge whose validity for its current state is secured. §2.3.2 reaches, from the object side, a verdict of the same family and greater strength — there, even an item’s membership in the body of knowledge comes out unsecured — and §2.4 diagnoses the common source.

2.3.2. Case II — Transformation of the Object#

Assume now that the transforming self is the object of self-knowledge — the known. As stated in §2.2, this case is neutral between (S1) and (S2).

One preliminary observation is needed, and it is peculiar to self-knowledge; the subject case did not require it. By (D1), the known is the knower. A knower is a bearer of knowledge: its body of knowledge belongs to its condition. In ordinary knowledge this is inert — the gained item resides in the knower, and the object known lies elsewhere, untouched by it. In self-knowledge, knower and known coincide, and so the gained item resides in the object of knowledge: every item of self-knowledge k, once gained, is part of the known. Call this the reflexivity of possession.

The two readings of (T) are now to be applied — and it will emerge that they sustain one and the same argument.

(i) Under (T-a): the object persists through the act, altered — and (T) specifies neither the respect nor the manner of the alteration. By the reflexivity of possession, the alteration may therefore reach the body of self-knowledge itself, the newly gained k included. Consider what (T)’s silence leaves open. (1) Accretion: the known merely grows — k is added, and every prior item remains invariant. (2) Substitution: k’s entry expels some item, one in and one out — and nothing excludes that the expelled item is k itself, admitted and expelled in the same stroke. (3) Interaction: k’s entry transforms the entire body of knowledge in some unspecified way. These are three among endlessly many possibilities, and (T) supplies no ground for excluding any of them. Hence, for every gained item and every subsequent time, it is undetermined whether the item persists unaltered, persists altered, or has been removed — undetermined, therefore, whether it is still possessed and whether it still holds; and every further act renews the indeterminacy.

Two conclusions follow. The first is a dilemma for the transformation theorist. If the alteration is read as mere accretion (1), then (T-a) collapses into the truism that learning adds knowledge, and the thesis loses exactly the radical content its proponents intend. If the alteration is left unspecified — as (T) leaves it — then no item’s survival in the known is determinable. The second conclusion is the verdict of the unspecified reading: the body of the known is at no time of determinable content — the known is, strictly, unknown. What remains knowable of the self is the pointlike actuality of the present act: the item k at the present moment, and it alone; and the next act subjects even this to the same indeterminacy. Accumulation is therefore impossible — and note where the failure falls. The self persists and every item is about it, so the condition that (K) states is met. Yet no growing body of knowledge arises, because no item determinably survives as an item. Under (T-a), then, satisfying (K) is not sufficient for accumulation: the failure lies on the side of the items, not on the side of the object. This verdict also strengthens the regress of Case I: there, an item’s validity for the subject’s current state was unsecured; here, its very membership in the known is unsecured.

(ii) Under (T-r): the object does not persist; the act replaces the known with a distinct successor. (For (S1) the successor cannot be a new organism — §2.3.1 closed that reading — and for no candidate may the replacement reach the target of the items, on pain of collapse into Case I, noted below; what the argument uses is replacement in the known’s constitution — the succeeding known is not, as constituted, its predecessor — and this is silent on inheritance.) This is not a different argument but the same one, with non-identity in the place of alteration. Non-identity, like alteration, is silent on constitution: it says that the successor is not the predecessor, and nothing about what the successor contains. By the reflexivity of possession, k belonged to the known that the act replaced; whether k — or any prior item — belongs to the succeeding known is therefore undetermined for exactly the reasons given under (i): inheritance with addition, inheritance with expulsion, and wholesale non-inheritance are all left open, among endlessly many possibilities, and (T) supplies no ground for excluding any of them. The indeterminacy verdict of (i) carries over unchanged, and so does the dilemma: read the succession as full inheritance plus one addition, and the thesis trivialises — the successor known is the old known plus one item, the very structure §3.2 will endorse as true of the self-conception; leave the succession unspecified, and no item’s survival is determinable.

One thing does not break here, and the guard placed on (K) in §2.1 is what shows it. (K)’s “object” is the target of the items — what they are about — and in self-knowledge every item is about the self. Case II confines the transformation to the known-pole of the schema, while the subject persists; the target of every item is that persisting subject; so all items are about one and the same self, and (K)’s condition is met under both readings. (Were the replacement instead read as reaching the target itself — the self as such — Case II would cease to be a separate case: by (D1), replacing the self replaces the knower, and the argument of Case I, where aboutness does break, has already disposed of that assignment.) Within Case II, the failure is retention, and retention alone — under alteration and under replacement alike.

The two readings therefore do not give two arguments but one: the reflexivity of possession, joined to (T)’s silence on what the change preserves, makes every item’s survival in the known undeterminable. (K) is satisfied throughout Case II, and accumulation fails nonetheless: satisfying (K) is not sufficient. That the two readings converge here is no accident, and §3.2 will show why: they are two descriptions of a single relation. The verdict of Case II: under (T) there is no accumulated body of self-knowledge, only the act of the moment, and the self one set out to know is never held as known.

2.3.3. Case III — Transformation without Subject-Object Distinction#

One might suspect that the difficulty is an artifact of imposing a subject/object structure on a situation that contains only one thing. So remove the structure. Assume only:

  1. there is no distinction between subject and object of knowledge — the knower and the known are one, and
  2. (T): the self is transformed through self-knowledge.

The problem returns unchanged. If the single self-identical entity changes whenever it knows itself, then — under (T-r) — no entity survives its own act of self-knowledge, and the argument of Case I goes through verbatim; and under either reading the entity’s body of self-knowledge belongs to the entity itself, so the indeterminacy argument of Case II goes through verbatim — as does, under (T-a), the regress of Case I, since the examining and the altered entity are here one and the same. Removing the subject/object distinction eliminates neither reading of (T) nor the conclusion drawn from it. The distinction was not the source of the paradox.

2.4. Diagnosis: the misassigned change#

The three cases exhaust the assignments of the transformation: to the subject, to the object, or to an undifferentiated knower-known. All three fail, and inspection of the arguments shows that each uses only (T) — including its silence on the respect and manner of change — together with (K), (D1), and what the concepts of knowledge and knower themselves supply. Case III shows that the subject/object distinction is not among the culprits, since the paradox survives its removal. (D1) is not negotiable: it defines the topic. (K) states what accumulation of knowledge is. What every failed case shares is (T), conjoined with demands internal to the concept of knowledge itself. There are three: aboutness — accumulating items must be about one and the same object (this is (K)); retention — an item, once gained, must remain part of the body of knowledge; and currency — an item’s bearing on the subject’s current state must admit of being secured. The breaks distribute by case, and each case breaks the demand of its own pole. Case I, where the transformation reaches the knower — which every item of self-knowledge is about — breaks aboutness under (T-r) and currency under (T-a). Case II, where the transformation is confined to the known-pole, breaks retention under both readings, while aboutness holds there throughout. In Case III the breaks of Cases I and II recur together. That retention breaks under both readings is a first sign that the two readings are one phenomenon — §3.2 confirms it. Every assignment of the transformation breaks at least one of these demands, and all the breaks trace to the same misassignment of the change.

Rejecting (T) outright is unattractive, because the phenomenon it registers is real: something plainly does change through the acquisition of self-knowledge. One’s self-conception after a successful act of self-knowledge is not what it was before. The way out is therefore not to deny that change, but to relocate it: to find a description under which what changes in self-knowledge is not the object of self-knowledge. If such a description exists, (T) can be retained as a truth about the set of self-conceptions — “set” in a sense §3.2 makes exact — while being rejected as a truth about the self; (K) is then satisfied because the reference never changes, and the demands of retention and currency are answered in the reformed shape §3.2 gives them.

The required description exists. It is Frege’s.

3. A Fregean Resolution#

To see why Frege’s distinction between sense and reference resolves the paradox, we first have to understand why identity statements can sometimes generate genuinely new knowledge.

3.1. Sense, Reference, and informative identity#

Frege’s “Über Sinn und Bedeutung” opens with the observation that a = a and a = b are statements of differing cognitive value: a = a “holds a priori and, according to Kant, is to be labelled analytic,” while true statements of the form a = b “often contain very valuable extensions of our knowledge and cannot always be established a priori.”5 The statement “the morning star is the morning star” is empty; the statement “the morning star is the evening star” records an astronomical discovery. Frege’s explanation: a sign has a reference (Bedeutung) — the object it designates — and also a sense (Sinn), which contains the object’s mode of presentation (die Art des Gegebenseins), the way in which the object is given. “Morning star” and “evening star” share one reference, the planet Venus, but differ in sense.

From this account, extract the structure of informative identity explicitly. For a statement a = b to be both true and informative:

(F1) the terms a and b must have one and the same reference — this is the truth condition; and

(F2) the terms must differ in sense — this is the informativeness condition.

a = a satisfies (F1) trivially and violates (F2); it is a tautology and yields nothing. The entire epistemic yield of an identity statement lies in difference of sense over sameness of reference.

State also what the yield is. A true informative identity a = b is knowledge about the reference: the object given in the mode contained in the sense of a is discovered to be the very object given in the mode contained in the sense of b. The reference is thereby discovered to admit a further mode of presentation. Every newly found sense of an object, bound to the existing ones by a true identity, extends knowledge of that object’s nature — this, and nothing else, is why a = b can be a discovery while a = a cannot. Note the two presuppositions this yield stands on: an identity can be informative only if there is a single reference object (F1), and only if that object can be given in more than one way (F2). These two presuppositions carry the remainder of the paper.

3.2. Self-knowledge as informative identity#

Map the problem of §2 onto Frege’s structure.

(D2) The reference of self-knowledge is the self — whichever candidate of §2.2 is installed.

(D3) A self-sense is a mode in which the self is given to itself. Each act of self-apprehension yields one such sense. Where convenient, write A, B, C, … for self-senses, and let A = B abbreviate: the self given in mode A is identical with the self given in mode B. (Strictly, the identity holds between the referents presented, not between the senses themselves; the abbreviation is harmless once this is fixed.)

(D4) An item of self-knowledge is a true identity statement of the form A = B — or, chained, A = B = C = … — asserting of distinct self-senses that their reference is one and the same. In natural language: “this, and this, and this are modes of me.”

Four consequences follow.

First, the paradox dissolves. What multiplied in Cases I–III was taken to be the self; under (D2)–(D4) what multiplies is the stock of its senses. (T) is retained in the only form the evidence for it ever supported: every act of self-apprehension changes the self-conception — it adds a sense to the stock. (T) is rejected in the form the paradox required: the reference does not change. (K) is satisfied, because every item of self-knowledge is about one and the same reference. The manifold of self-concepts that in §2 was “mutually unconnected because the object of reference has changed” is now connected, and connected by something truth-apt: the identity chain, whose truth is grounded in sameness of reference.

Second, a single self-sense is not yet self-knowledge. By (F2), the epistemic yield of identity requires at least two senses. A single mode of self-givenness, taken alone, is the analogue of a single term: there is nothing informative to assert of it except A = A, a tautology. Self-knowledge begins with the second sense and the first informative identity. This will be sharpened in §3.4.

Third, growth of self-knowledge is well-defined. To gain self-knowledge is to discover a new self-sense and to extend the identity chain by a true link. The chain A = B = C = … grows; the reference stands. This is the exact structure of Frege’s astronomical case: the discovery that the object given as the morning star is the object given as the evening star extended knowledge of Venus without multiplying Venus.

Fourth, both readings of (T) can now be given their due — and the set-theoretic form of the relocated object shows exactly how. §2.4 claimed that (T) can be retained as a truth about the set of self-conceptions; the claim can be made precise. Let Sₙ be the stock of self-senses gathered up to a given act, considered as a set, and let the act contribute a new sense N, so that the resulting stock is Sₙ₊₁ = Sₙ ∪ {N}. Two elementary facts follow. First, Sₙ₊₁ ≠ Sₙ: by extensionality, sets with different members are distinct objects, and so the self-conception that results from the act is not identical with the self-conception that entered it. This is (T-r), word for word — true, but true of the stock of senses, not of their reference. Second, Sₙ ⊊ Sₙ₊₁: the earlier stock is properly contained in its successor; nothing is lost, everything is carried over, exactly one thing is added. Strictly, no set alters — the earlier stock is not modified but succeeded — so what (T-a) registered was not a second phenomenon but the same one under a different description: replacement under proper inclusion wears the guise of alteration. The two readings that divided the case analysis of §2 are thus the two conjuncts of a single relation, and (T) is doubly true of the self-conception and doubly false of the self. (The subset relation is secure because senses, being dated by their acts, are superseded but never removed; and a masquerading representation expelled from the chain was, by §3.4, never a sense, so the set never contained it.)

What remains of the alteration reading are its two complaints. From the object side (Case II, under both readings): the gained item, being part of the known, may be altered or expelled by the very transformation it triggers, or simply not inherited by the succeeding known — so that no item’s survival is determinable. From the subject side (the regress of Case I): no characterization of the subject’s current state is ever secured. Both are answered by one feature of (D4). A self-sense is dated by the act that yields it; an item of self-knowledge is an identity between dated modes; and such an identity, once true, is true timelessly — not because the self is always given in the same way, but because sameness of reference cannot decay: what varies over time is how the self is given to itself — the succession of its dated modes — never the fact that each of them presents it.6 The first complaint thereby loses its licence. What powered Case II was (T)’s silence on what the change preserves: accretion, substitution, self-expulsion, global interaction, and non-inheritance were all left open, so no item’s survival in the known was determinable. Under the reformed description the manner is not open but fixed — Sₙ ⊊ Sₙ₊₁, growth by proper inclusion — and fixed not by stipulation but as a consequence of (D4): the members of the body of self-knowledge are timeless truths, and no later act can falsify, transform, or expel one — and the succeeding stock demonstrably contains its predecessor, which answers the replacement reading’s form of the complaint identically. Exactly the benign scenario that the transformation theorist could not assume without trivialising his thesis is here derived; retention is secured, and the pointlike actuality that Case II left standing as the sole knowable — the yield of the present act — is preserved as a dated, timelessly true link. The second complaint finds no target: under (D4) there is no stock of standing present-tense characterizations whose validity would need re-securing; items of self-knowledge were never of that form. What remains of the demand to know the self one currently is, is met in the only way anything temporal is known as it currently is — by a fresh act, yielding a fresh dated mode. A characterization of how one now is that would stand secured independently of any act is something no temporal object affords; its absence marks no special defect of self-knowledge, and the appearance that it did was the paradox.

3.3. The fixed reference#

The application in §3.2 requires that something hold fixed while the senses multiply. This section states what that fixity amounts to, and establishes a proposition that the informal problem of §2 obscured.

Proposition. The subject of self-knowledge is not identical with the object of self-knowledge.

The object of any given act of self-knowledge is a self-sense, or an identity among self-senses (D4). The subject — the self in its role as reference — is not a sense. This is a category distinction, not a discovery: a reference is what senses are senses of; if the reference occurred as the content of an act of self-apprehension, it would occur as a mode of presentation, that is, as one more sense, and what it is a sense of would remain in the reference position. The reference therefore never appears among the contents of self-knowledge. It stands to those contents as their condition, not as one of them. In this functional respect the reference is transcendental — a condition of the knowing relation that is not an item within it — and it is transcendental in this sense regardless of which candidate of §2.2 is installed. This is Wittgenstein’s line in the Tractatus: “The philosophical I is not the man, not the human body or the human soul of which psychology treats, but the metaphysical subject, the limit—not a part of the world” (5.641); and, of the eye and the field of sight: “you do not really see the eye,” and “from nothing in the field of sight can it be concluded that it is seen from an eye” (5.633).7 The point is structural, and it applies to (S1) exactly as to (S2): even the embodied individual, installed as reference, does not occur as content of self-knowledge — what occurs as content are its senses.

The Proposition does not contradict (D1): “object of knowledge” is ambiguous between what the knowledge is about and what is given as content in an act. What self-knowledge is about is settled at the level of reference, and there (D1) holds — the knowledge is about the very self that knows. What an act grasps is settled at the level of sense, and there the Proposition holds — no content of self-knowledge is its reference. The subject/object schema of §2 could not draw this distinction; the schema is accordingly not mapped onto Frege’s distinction but superseded by it, and “subject” and “object” occur in this section only as labels for the two levels.

Stability requirement. The reference must be stable — unchanged across all acts of self-knowledge. This is not an additional stipulation; it is forced by the case analysis. Any account on which the reference changes between acts reinstates Cases I–III, whichever pole the change is assigned to. So: a stable, unchangeable reference is a necessary condition of any coherent account of accumulating self-knowledge. Three qualifications fix its status. (a) The fixity required is functional: it is a condition on whatever occupies the reference role, not a substantive doctrine about an unchanging soul-substance. Nothing here licenses the inference from “the reference role requires fixity” to “there exists a simple, immutable entity.” (b) The warrant for the fixed reference is structural, not empirical: §2 established that a fixed reference is a necessary condition of coherent, accumulating self-knowledge, so the reference is secured by inference to a necessary condition — and this is the sense in which it is a priori. Correspondingly, what is known of it is that it stands and that it is one; every what belongs to the sense side. (c) For candidate (S2) the fixity is immediate: the transcendental I is not a bearer of content — every content belongs to the sense side — so there is nothing along which it could change, and it satisfies the requirement trivially. For candidate (S1) the fixity is the numerical identity of the individual through qualitative change — which is all the requirement demands, since the requirement concerns the reference, not its states.

Both candidates therefore serve. (S1) as reference: self-knowledge is anchored in a material object, and the object of self-knowledge is the way that individual is given to itself. (S2) as reference: the transcendental I stands as the fixed pole. The bare I am is not this pole, and not a separate presentation alongside the others: it is the primitive sense in its purest form — the form every primitive mode carries, since each primitive presentation is a presentation-as-mine (§3.4). The manifold of presentations of how one is accumulates around the fixed pole, and combined they yield “I am this,” “I am that,” and finally “I am so, and so, and so….” The resolution is indifferent between the two candidates, exactly as the paradox was.

3.4. Primitive modes and derived representations#

One feature distinguishes the reflexive application from Frege’s astronomical case, and it must be stated rather than hidden. The senses of Venus are public: many minds can grasp “the morning star,” and for Frege this publicity is definitional of sense as against the private idea (Vorstellung). The way the self is given to itself is not public. Frege concedes this himself in “Der Gedanke”: “everyone is presented to himself in a particular and primitive way, in which he is presented to no-one else.”8 The concession stands in tension with his own doctrine of the publicity of sense, and the subsequent literature on the first person has worked over exactly this fault line.9 One could press the tension further and argue that in the reflexive case no mode of presentation is interposed at all — that self-presence is medium-less. The present argument neither requires nor takes that step.10 What it requires is only the claim contained verbatim in Frege’s sentence: the self is given to itself in a primitive way — primitive in the sense of underived, not formed from anything else, and available only to its owner.

This primitivity is not one mode’s privilege among others; it is the mark of self-givenness as such, and the point is a consequence of (D3), not an added assumption. By (D3), a self-sense is a mode in which the self is given to itself — and givenness is underived: what is constructed is not given but made. Every self-sense is therefore primitive.11 What one might be tempted to call “derived senses of the self” — representations formed by inference from one’s own behaviour, adopted from the attributions of others, or composed in self-interpretation — are not senses at all: they are derived representations about the self. They are not constituents of self-knowledge. They have exactly one role in the account: they are its possibilities of failure, actualized when a derived representation is taken for a primitive sense.

A single primitive self-presentation is therefore best classified not as knowledge but as evidence: it is given only to its owner, and by (F2) it supports, taken alone, nothing beyond the tautology A = A. Self-knowledge begins at the first informative identity, and the terms of every such identity are primitive modes: it is from primitive presentations — which are contentful — that all informative growth of self-knowledge stems. Derived representations contribute nothing constitutive. A report from another may prompt discovery, by directing attention to a mode not yet noticed; but what then enters the chain is the primitive presentation the report occasions, never the report itself. A generalization such as “I am so-and-so in the mornings” is likewise no sense but a summary, whose truth supervenes on the chain of dated modes it compresses. Before such contents are redeemed by primitive givenness they are, at best, knowledge about the person — knowledge of the third-personal kind that anyone could have of her — and not self-knowledge, which runs through givenness alone. The difficulty of gaining self-knowledge is located exactly here: a derived representation can present itself with the felt immediacy of givenness — the confabulated mode is the standing example — and nothing in a content’s form guarantees the difference. §3.5 states this as a failure condition.

3.5. Truth conditions and failure (masquerade)#

Truth conditions. By (D4) and (F1): the statement A = B = C = … is true if and only if each of A, B, C, … is a sense of one and the same reference. This immediately yields a determinate failure mode.

Failure. By §3.4, every term that belongs in the chain is a primitive mode, and a primitive mode is a sense of the self by its very character: it cannot occur in the chain as a term that fails to present the self. Failure therefore has exactly one entry point: masquerade — a derived representation about the self, taken for a primitive sense and inserted into the chain as if it were a mode of givenness. An attribution adopted from others, a confabulated mode, a self-interpretation that answers to nothing in the self’s givenness: none of these is a sense of the self, and each can be mistaken for one. Let B and C be primitive modes, and let A be such an inserted representation. Then A = B is false, and the chain A = B = C is false. The attempt at self-knowledge fails, and there is a fact of the matter that it fails. Note what grounds the classification: a term belongs to the chain if and only if it is a primitive mode of givenness — the chain is composed of, not merely anchored in, the primitively given. And since a derived representation can carry the felt immediacy of givenness, while nothing in a content’s form betrays the difference, the acquisition of self-knowledge passes constantly through the possibility of masquerade: this is the precise location of its difficulty. Self-knowledge is therefore fallible and non-arbitrary: the identity chain can be extended wrongly, and a wrong extension is false, not merely infelicitous. Any account on which the composition of a self-conception cannot fail is, by that very feature, not an account of knowledge.

4. Against Narrative Identity without a Substrate#

Narrative accounts of personal identity hold that the self is constituted by the story that binds its episodes; in Ricœur’s terms, identity in the sense of ipse — selfhood — “implies no assertion concerning some unchanging core of the personality,” so that no permanence of a substrate (idem) is required.12 Dennett’s “center of narrative gravity” is the limiting case: a narrative self with no fixed referent at all.13 Against every account of this family, the following argument.

  1. The narrative act consists in binding presentations A, B, C, … into one identity: “these are modes of one and the same me.” Its formal content is the chain A = B = C = ….
  2. By (F1), that chain has truth conditions only if there is a single reference object of which A, B, C are senses.
  3. By hypothesis, the substrate-free account supplies no such object.
  4. Therefore, on that account, the chain has no truth conditions: it is not truth-apt, and a fortiori cannot be known.
  5. A composition that cannot be true or false cannot fail; by the failure criterion above, it is not knowledge but stipulation. The narrative connection of A, B, C becomes an arbitrary act of composing an identity, with no epistemic gain.

Hence the dilemma for narrative identity without a substrate: either the account tacitly presupposes a fixed referent — in which case it is the present account, with the reference unacknowledged — or it genuinely dispenses with one, in which case self-narration forfeits every claim to be self-knowledge. The scope of this result should be stated precisely: it does not show that narrative practice is worthless or that selves are not articulated narratively — §3.2 asserts that they are, since the identity chain is the narrative’s formal skeleton. It shows that narrative construction constitutes knowledge only under conditions (F1) and (F2): a reference object, and a multiplicity of its senses. Narration is the form of self-knowledge; the fixed reference is its truth-maker.

5. Conclusion#

The senses of the self are not given all at once; the chain A = B = C = … is built link by link, over time. Self-knowledge, then, is not the pursuit of an object that the pursuit itself displaces — that description was the paradox, and it rested on a misassignment of the change. Nor is it the free composition of a story — that description forfeits truth. It is the discovery, over time, of the many modes in which one and the same self is given, bound into knowledge by identities that are informative precisely because the modes are many and true precisely because the self is one.
